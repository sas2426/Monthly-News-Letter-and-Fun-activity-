SkyWatch Across the Globe: Earth's Slowest, Farthest Month

Space · Aerospace · Science · Research — every July, Earth drifts to the farthest point of its orbit and slows to its laziest pace of the year. Here's everything happening above us this month.

Segment 01 — SkyWatch Highlights

What to look up for this month

July's sky is quiet in a specific way: Earth is at its most distant and slowest-moving point, which is why the whole Northern Hemisphere gets its longest season. Here's the observing calendar.

JUL 4 · BEFORE SUNRISE

Mars–Uranus conjunction

The two planets pass within a fraction of a degree of each other in Gemini — one of the closest pairings of the pair until 2053. Uranus is faint, so you'll want binoculars; Mars sits just below the Pleiades as a helpful signpost.

binoculars recommended
JUL 6 · 1:30 PM IST-ADJ.

Earth at aphelion

Earth reaches 152,087,774 km from the Sun — about 5 million km farther than in January. It's still peak Northern summer, proof that seasons come from axial tilt, not distance.

no equipment needed
JUL 11–12 · DAWN

Moon, Mars, Saturn & Uranus parade

The Moon walks past Saturn, Mars, Venus, Jupiter and Neptune at different points through the month — before sunrise on the 11th and 12th, all four line up in the eastern sky together.

naked eye + binoculars
AROUND JUL 14 · NEW MOON

Milky Way & Comet 10P/Tempel 2

New Moon skies make mid-July the best dark-sky window of the month for the galactic core — and for spotting Comet 10P/Tempel 2, a fuzzy 8th-magnitude glow near Capricornus, brightening toward its August close approach.

dark sky site ideal
JUL 30–31 · POST-MIDNIGHT

Southern Delta Aquariids peak

Faint, fast meteors radiating from Aquarius — but 2026's peak falls right after a near-full Buck Moon, so rates will be washed out. Best strategy: watch the moon-free hours the week before.

moonlight will interfere
JUL 30–31 · BONUS

Alpha Capricornids, same night

A minor shower overlapping the Aquariids — low count, but known for bright, slow fireballs that can occasionally punch through moonlight.

watch for fireballs
Segment 02 — Aerospace & Missions

Four spacecraft, four kinds of daring

July 2026 is a genuinely busy month in mission operations — a telescope rescue, two asteroid encounters, and continued lunar groundwork.

NASA · JUL 2026 (LAUNCH IN PROGRESS)

🇺🇸Swift Boost: a robotic rescue

NASA's Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory has been losing altitude faster than expected due to rising solar activity. Katalyst Space's robotic LINK spacecraft is attempting to launch on a Pegasus XL rocket from Kwajalein Atoll to grapple Swift and tow it to a stable orbit — the first time a commercial robotic spacecraft has attempted to capture a satellite never designed to be serviced. The first launch attempt was scrubbed on July 2; teams are working toward the next opportunity.

JAXA · JUL 5, 2026

🇯🇵Hayabusa2# flies by asteroid Torifune

Fresh off its Ryugu sample return, Hayabusa2 is attempting a high-speed flyby of near-Earth asteroid (98943) Torifune — passing as close as 1–10 km from its surface at 5.25 km/s. It's an engineering test for precision close approaches that could one day help deflect a hazardous asteroid.

CNSA · JUL 4, 2026

🇨🇳Tianwen-2 closes in on Kamoʻoalewa

China's asteroid sample-return probe, already parked near quasi-moon 469219 Kamoʻoalewa since early June, closes to within 20 km this month for its first detailed imaging pass. Scientists are racing to settle a real debate: is Kamoʻoalewa an ordinary asteroid, or a fragment blasted off the Moon itself?

ONGOING

🌕Commercial lunar groundwork continues

Commercial landers and rover programs continue integration and testing work this month, laying groundwork for upcoming Moon surface deployments later in the decade.

Segment 03 — Science & Research

What the data is telling us

GAMMA-RAY BURSTS

SVOM keeps watch

The French–Chinese SVOM observatory continues rapid-response monitoring of gamma-ray bursts and other high-energy transients, feeding follow-up campaigns across ground- and space-based telescopes.

EARLY UNIVERSE

JWST and early barred galaxies

Ongoing James Webb Space Telescope surveys keep turning up barred spiral galaxies at surprisingly early cosmic times — pushing back estimates of when galactic structure like ours first took shape.

RADIO ASTRONOMY

SKA construction advances

Work continues on the Square Kilometre Array across its South African and Australian sites — the future giant radio observatory built to map cosmic magnetic fields across the Universe.

  • Mark the Sun off-center, toward one end.
  • Mark Earth's July position — farthest from the Sun, moving slowest.
